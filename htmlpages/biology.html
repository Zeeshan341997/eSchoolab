Digestive System

To Identify the system in the given dissected frog.
Frog's Anatomy
Alimentary Canal

Alimentary Canal of frog is a long coiled tube of varying diameter, extending from mouth to the cloacal aperture. It consists of

Mouth

It is the anterior opening of the alimentary canal lies between two semi circular jaws.

Buccal Cavity (Buccopharyngeal Cavity)

Posteriorly mouth opens into buccal cavity which is guarded by upper fixed & lower movable jaw.

  • Upper jaw is fused with skull and have sharped pointed curved maxillary teeth.
  • Lower jaw is teethless, movabel and attached to the skull.
  • Internal nostrils are two small openings on the roof,behind the tip of snout.
  • Vomerine teeth are two groups (patches) of teeth on the roof of buccal cavity behind the internal nostrils.
  • Bulging of eyes on the roof formed by the in pushing of the eyes.
  • Opening of Eustachian tubes are two small openings one on either side of the roof of the buccal cavity close to the angle of jaws.
  • Tongue is a fleshy bilobed sticky structure lying in the floor, attached anteriorly and free posteriorly.
    • Functions
  • Sticky tongue present in buccal cavity is used for capturing of prey.
  • Teeth present in buccal cavity are used to hold prey.

Pharynx

Buccal Cavity narrows posteriorly to form pharynx. It has following structures and openings.

  • Opening of vocal sacs are two small openings one on either side of the floor in the angle of lower jaw (male frogs only)
  • Glottis is a vertical slit in the middle of floor of buccal cavity.
  • Opening of gullet is a wide posterior opening behind the glottis.
    • Functions
    Pharynx acts as passage for food.
Oesophagus

It is a short and muscular tube with glandular walls. Due to the absence of neck it is a short tube.
Functions

  • It connects pharynx to the stomach.
  • Oesophagus leading from the mouth cavity to the stomach.
  • Glandular secretion and peristaltic movement transport food to the stomach.

Stomach

Oesophagus opens into a thick walled muscular sac like structure called stomach. Anterior wide end of stomach is called cardiac end and posterior narrow end of stomach is called pyloric end.
Functions

  • Muscular contractions and relaxations of stomach wall break food into small pieces.
  • HCL secreted by the stomach soften the food.
  • HCL convert pepsinogen into pepsin.
  • Pepsing helps in digestion of proteins.

Small Intestine

Pyloric end of stomach opens into a long narrow coiled tube small intestine which can be distinguished into duodenum and ileum.

Duodenum

It is the anterior part of the small intestine which form 'U' shape with the stomach. Hepatopancreatic duct from liver and pancreas open into it.
Functions

  • In duodenum bile mixed with food and make it slightly basic.
  • In duodenum, pancreatic juice mixed with the food and convert it into diffusible form.

    • Ileum

    It is the posterior coiled part of the small intestine which connects the duodenum to the large intestine (Rectum). Inner lining of the walls of ileum is raised in the form of longitudinal folds (villi).
    Functions

    • Food digest in the ileum by the action of enzymes.
    • Digested food absorb through villi of the ileum.

    Rectum

    Ileum open into a short and wide tube called rectum. It opens to the exterior at the cloacal aperture.
    Functions

    • It absorbs water.
    • It stores undigested food.

    Cloaca

    It is the posterior opening of the alimentary canal.
    Functions

    • Undigested food expelled out through the cloaca.

    Digestive Glands
    Digestive Glands
    Liver

    It is the largest gland of the body. It is dark brown in color and consists of two lobes in which left is incompletely divided into two.

    Gall Bladder

    It is a bluish rounded sac on the under surface of the right lobe of liver. Liver secrets bile which is stored in gall bladder.

    Bile Duct

    It is narrow, thin light coloured rube tube formed by the union of the hepatic ducts coming from the lobes of liver passing through the pancreas from which it receives pancreatic duct and opens in the duodenum as hepatopancreatic duct. Bile passes into the duodenum through hepatopancretic duct.

    Functions

    • It secretes bile.
    • Bile neutralize acidic food.
    • Bile emulsifies fats.

    Pancreas

    It is situated between stomach and duodenum. It is leaf like and secrets a secretion called pancreatic juice which passes into the duodenum through hepatopancreatic duct.
    Functions

    • Pancreas secretes digesticve enzymes.
    • Theses enzymes make food diffusible.

    Pulmonary Respiration

    To Identify the system in the given dissected frog.
    Frog's Respiratory
    Alimentary Canal
    Description & Functions of Organs

    Pulmonary respiratory system consists of following parts.

    • External & Internal nostrils
    • Buccal Cavity
    • Glottis
    • Laryngo-tracheal chamber
    • Bronchus
    • A pair of lungs

    External Nares (Nostrils)

    There are two openings located at the anterior dorsal side of snout. They open into the buccal cavity through internal nares.
    Functions
    The serve as passage of air in and out of the buccal cavity.

    Internal Nostrils

    These two openings lie on the roof of the buccal cavity.
    Functions
    The serve as passage of air in and out of the buccal cavity.

    Buccal Cavity

    Internal nostrils open into a wide buccal cavity which is guarded by upper immoveable and lower moveable jaw.
    Functions
    Since frog uses positive pressure breathing mechanism so the buccal cavity plays an important role in respiration and provide passage for air.

    Glottis

    It is a vertical slit in the middle of floor of buccal cavity.
    Functions
    It serves as an opening to Laryngo-tracheal chamber.

    Laryngotracheal Chamber

    This chamber formed by the fusion of larynx or organ of voice and trachea or wind pipe. It si small thin walled chamber. Its walls are supported by a pair of arytenoid cortilages and ring like cricoid. Posteriorly it opens into the lungs through glottis.
    Functions
    It serves as an air passage.

    Bronchus

    It is a pair of tubes formed by the division of laryngotracheal chamber. Each bronchus enters into the lung of corresponding side.
    Functions
    They also provide passage of the air.

    Lungs

    Lungs are balloon shaped and located in the anterior part of the body cavity on either side of the heart. Lungs are pink in colour, thin walled and spongy. Their outer surface is smooth. They are richly supplied with capillaries. Each bronchus after entering into the lung divides and form alveoli or air sac.
    Functions

    • They absorb oxygen from the inhaled air.
    • They excrete carbondioxide produced in the body.
    • The excrete water vapours.

    Photosynthesis

    To prove using aquatic plant that oxygen gas is evolved during photosynthesis.
    Theory Requirements Method Observation Result Precautions
    Theory
    Photosynthesis

    It is a biochemical process by which green part of plant manufacture their own food (Glucose) C6H12O6 by combining carbondioxide and water in the presence of sunlight and chlorophyll. During this process oxygen is liberated.
    OR
    The process of building of sugar from carbondioxide and water by green parts of the plants using energy from sunlight which is absorbed by chlorophyll is known as photosynthesis.

    Equation
    6CO2 + 6H2O       C6H12O6+6O2
    Requirements
    Beaker
    Funnel
    Test Tube
    Sodium Bicarbonate
    Water
    Hydrilla
    Method
    • Place a beaker filled with water.
    • Place some cut branches of Hydrilla (an aquatic plant) under cover of a funnel in a beaker filled with a water.
    • Add pinch of sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) as a source of carbondioxide.
    • Invert a test tube over a funnel filled with water. (Take a test tube filled with water, place your thumb on the mouth of test tube and invert it over the stem of the funnel.
    • Expose the apparatus to bright sunlight.
    Observation

    Aftter some time bubbles of gas appear from the plant and collect in the inverted test tube by downward displacement of water. When sufficient gas has accumulated, the test tube is removed and a glowing match stick is inserted in it which burns with a flame.

    Result

    This experiment shows that oxygen gas is evolved during photosynthesis.

    Precautions
    • Stem of funnel must be completely dipped in water.
    • There must be no air bubbles in the test tube at the start of the experiment.

    Transpiration

    To prove using aquatic plant that oxygen gas is evolved during photosynthesis.
    Theory Requirements Method Observation Result Precautions
    Theory

    Plants absorb large quantity of water from the soil by means of root hairs. A small quantity of water is retain in the plant body for photosynthesis while most part of it lost through stomata of the leaves in the form of water vapours. The process by which plant lost water as water vapours from the aerial part of the plant is called transpiration.

    Cobalt Chloride Method

    Requirements
    Potted Plant
    Cobalt Chloride Paper
    Plane Glass Slides
    Iron Stand
    Paper Clip
    Rubber Band
    Method
    • Take a potted plant.
    • Place blue and dry cobalt chloride paper one on the upper surface and one on the lower surface of leaf.
    • Cover both cobalt chloride papers with plane glass slides and tie the slides with transpiration clip.
    • Hold the clip with iron stand.
    • Leave the apparatus undisturbed for few minutes
    • After some time remove cobalt chloride papers from the leaf and observe their colours.
    Observation
    • Blue cobalt chloride paper turns pink.
    • Blur cobalt chloride paper of lower surface turns more pink as compared to upper surface because of large number of stomata on the lower surface.
    Result
    • Transpiration takes place through leaves.
    • Rate of transpiration is higher on the lower surface.
    • Lower surface of the leaf have greater number of stomata.
    Requirements Method Observation Result

    Bell Jar Method

    Requirements
    Potted Plant
    Bell Jar
    Polythene Bag
    Method
    • Take a potted plant.
    • Cover the pot and its soil completely by polythene bag to prevent evaporation of water.
    • Cover the plant with bell jar and leave the apparatus in sunlight for sometime.
    Observation

    After sometime water drops will be seen on the inner surface of bell jar.

    Result

    Transpiration takes place through leaves of the plant.

    Precautions
    • The plant must be placed at a place where it can receive maximum amount of light.
    • Cobalt Chloride paper must be completely dry or without moisture at the start of the expermient.
